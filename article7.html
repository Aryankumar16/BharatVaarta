Rise of the Mughal Empire

The Mughal Empire has been one of the greatest and most important empires in Indian history. Emerging in the 16th century, this empire managed to thrive until almost the end of the third century. Its conquest was indeed characterized by various remarkable rulers who had an indelible mark left on the Indian subcontinent.

The Founder: Babur

The founder of the Mughal Empire was Babur, who came from the lineage of Timur and Genghis Khan. Babur, a good military leader as well as a poet, invaded India in 1526 and defeated the Delhi Sultanate at the Battle of Panipat. His victory marked the beginning of Mughal rule in India.

The Consolidation of Power: Humayun

The next emperor after Babur was his son Humayun. His period was quite stormy. He lost his kingdom to Sher Shah Suri and, in his turn, went to seek refuge in Persia. Once back on the throne, Humayun laid the base of the unification of the Mughal empire.

The Golden Age: Akbar the Great

Akbar the Great, son of Humayun, is considered one of the greatest emperors in Mughal history. He added to the empire's expansion, brought about reforms, and supported religious tolerance. His period was a golden age for the Mughal Empire, as his reign indicated peace, prosperity, and cultural flourishing. He also introduced the Din-i-Ilahi, a syncretic religion that united Hindus and Muslims.

The Pinnacle of Power: Jahangir

Jahangir, son of Akbar, continued in the line of his father. A patron of arts and culture, he was the great patron of Mughal painting and miniature art flourished under his reign. The reign of Jahangir saw a period relatively peaceful and stable enough for the empire to flourish well.

The Glory of Shah Jahan

Among Shah Jahan's architectural feats, most famous perhaps were his works with his father being Jahangir. Shah Jahan commissioned the building of Taj Mahal; this lovely mausoleum built in the remembrance of his wife who he loves so dearly named Mumtaz Mahal. In his time also were these architectural masterpieces: Red Fort and the Jama Masjid in Delhi. These architectural works drained the economic power of the empire from the extravagant and ambitious plans of Shah Jahan.

The Decline: Aurangzeb

He was an orthodox Muslim and son of Shah Jahan, following very strict Islamic laws. All non-Muslims were punished and tortured, causing deep resentment and rebellion all around. His aggressive policies, along with military campaigns against other kingdoms, weakened the empire's resources.

The Mughal Empire has left an indelible mark on the Indian subcontinent, though it ended in decline. Its ruling elite, especially Akbar, Jahangir, and Shah Jahan, created a magnificent cultural heritage that inspires and amazes people. Architecture, literature, painting, and culinary arts are the areas influenced by the Mughal Empire, which have given a deep impact on the Indian culture.
